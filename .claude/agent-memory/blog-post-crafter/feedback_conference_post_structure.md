Patterns pulled from Rafael's edited version of the ICLR 2026 keynote post (commit 4c91ffa). These are structural moves, not sentence-level style, and should shape the next conference post.

## Title pattern: "Notes from [Venue]: [Speaker] on [Topic]"

The final title: `Notes from ICLR 2026: Maja Mataric on What AI Still Needs from Bodies`.

**Why:** The "Notes from..." framing is humble and attributional. It matches the conference-attribution rule: the post is Rafael's notes on someone else's talk, not his own thesis. A clever or editorialized title would overclaim.

**How to apply:** Default conference-post title to `Notes from [Venue Year]: [Speaker] on [Sharp framing of their central claim]`. The second half should still be specific and sharp, just not pretending to be Rafael's own argument.

## Section headers: declarative claims, not topic labels

The edited H2s are complete thoughts, often declarative statements the speaker made:
- `The question she opened with`
- `Intelligence evolved inside a body. Ours didn't come from text.`
- `The machines people will actually accept`
- `Why the body keeps mattering`
- `The ending she chose`

**Why:** A header that states a claim pulls the reader forward and previews the argument. A header that labels a topic ("On embodiment", "Personality") is inert.

**How to apply:** When drafting headers, write the claim the section makes or the question it answers, as a short sentence or noun phrase with a verb. Two-sentence H2s are acceptable when rhythm calls for it (see the "Intelligence evolved..." example).

## Closing: end on the speaker, not the author

The final line of the edited post: `She closed on one line, and I'll end on it too: *let's think about enhancing, rather than replacing.*`

**Why:** For conference posts, the speaker's words are the point. Ending on their quote (rather than an author reflection, teaser, or "what I took away" paragraph) keeps attribution clean and lets the idea land on its own.

**How to apply:** When the speaker has a memorable closing line, end the post there. Introduce it with a brief handoff ("She closed on...", "Her final slide said...") and give the quoted line its own paragraph in italics. Do not add a reflective paragraph after it.

## Verbatim speaker quotes: italicized, cited as such

When Rafael reproduces a speaker's phrasing verbatim, he marks it explicitly and italicizes it:
- `a sentence I wrote down verbatim: *we know what we should do, but we don't do it.*`
- `A slide simply asked: *how do we create machines that people will accept?*`
- `Then a slide that I suspect will get quoted a lot: *foundation models can't understand affect.*`

**Why:** Transparent attribution. The reader knows which words are the speaker's and which are Rafael's paraphrase.

**How to apply:** Use `*italics*` for reproduced speaker phrases and slide text. Introduce with a handoff phrase that names the source ("a sentence", "a slide", "she said"). Do not use blockquotes for single lines; reserve those for longer quoted passages if they appear.

## Image captions: short, factual, italicized

Caption style observed:
- `*The main hall half an hour before doors. You can tell a conference is serious about a talk when they start setting up the overflow seating before breakfast.*`
- `*Title slide. USC Viterbi and Google DeepMind.*`
- `*The CBT study (Mina Kian et al., arXiv 2402.17937).*`

One or two sentences, italicized, factual. A light observation or specific detail is welcome; flourish or editorialization is not. Citations (arXiv IDs, author names) go in parentheses inside the caption when relevant.
